Research Projects (at Boston University)

Peer and Authority Pressure in Information Propagation Models

Existing models of information diffusion assume that peer influence is the main reason for observed propagation patterns. In this work, we examined the role of authority pressure on the observed information cascades, by characterizing some nodes in the network as “authority” nodes. In collaboration with my mentor, I developed a model that associates every information item with two parameters that quantify the impact of peer and authority pressure on its propagation. I then implemented functions to learn these parameters given a network and the observed diffusion patterns of the item, and characterize the item as peer- or authority-propagated, by setting up and solving a convex optimization problem. My advisor and I also developed a randomization test that evaluates the statistical significance of our findings and makes our item characterization robust to noise. I applied this method to real data from online media and scientific-collaboration networks, and found that there is a strong signal of authority pressure in these networks.

This work resulted in a publication:

  • Aris Anagnostopoulos, George Brova, Evimaria Terzi: “Peer and authority pressure in information-propagation models”, Principles and Practice of Knowledge Discovery in Databases (ECML-PKDD) 2011. [code]

Graph-Agnostic Identification of Influence Relationships

Although our work on peers and authorities assumed that peer connections are known, this information is missing in many real applications. For example, our Yahoo! collaborator pointed out that Yahoo! knows the times when users clicked on an advertisement or adopted a product but remains unaware of the social relationships amongst its users. My current research focuses on identifying the roles of individuals in a network without knowing the structure of the network itself, and using the activation times of individuals with respect to items as clues. This work is in collaboration with my mentor at Boston University and Kun Liu, who is a research scientist at Yahoo!.

Query by Humming

Query-by-humming (QBH) enables users to identify a song by humming or singing a section of it. While existing work and commercial applications rely on a collection of query samples for each song in their database, our approach does not require any training queries. My primary contribution to this project was to define a new measure of edit distance between two melodies that tolerates mistakes commonly made by amateur singers. For example, many people shorten pauses in songs, and sing slightly off tune, such that the difference in pitch between two successive notes is accurate even if each note by itself is off-key. Therefore, my edit distance measure penalizes such mistakes less heavily. I also defined a multi-dimensional time series format in which to represent both the library of songs (which consist of many tracks, each corresponding to different instruments or vocals) and the hummed queries (which only contain information about the notes hummed), so that the two can be meaningfully compared. I implemented the above metrics, as well as a Fast Fourier Transform algorithm to convert a sung query into a sequence of notes, to create a functioning query-by-humming system. I also wrote a web crawler to create a large collection of test queries, which I used to evaluate the accuracy of the system.
